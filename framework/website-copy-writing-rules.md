WEBSITE CONTENT GENERATION FRAMEWORK
(Based on KILR Principles & User‑Journey Optimisation)
PURPOSE
Provide a structured process for generating clear, engaging, user‑centric website content.
Guide visitors seamlessly through their journey, addressing needs and objections at each stage.
Deliver concise, impactful copy that drives users toward the page’s primary goal.
CORE PRINCIPLES (All are non‑negotiable)
3.1 Clarity & Conciseness – Use plain language and focused paragraphs. Purge every unnecessary word.
3.2 Benefit‑Oriented – Lead with emotional and practical outcomes; features only support.
3.3 Audience‑Centric – Speak directly to the reader’s pain points, desires, and decision stage.
3.4 Solution‑Focused – Demonstrate precisely how the offer solves the user’s problem or fulfils a goal.
3.5 Conversational & Provocative – Language must be conversational and easy to read, never stiff or overly professional. Use strong, confronting words that provoke thought and fire the imagination while remaining brand‑aligned.
3.6 Minimal Company Name Repetition – Never open with “At [company]”; mention only when essential.
3.7 Professionalism – Avoid jargon unless defined; ban puns, gimmicks, and excess informality.
3.8 Australian Spelling & Localisation – Default to AU English unless Brand Guide states otherwise.
3.9 Respectful Language – Never instruct or guilt the reader. Avoid telling them how to feel or act (e.g., “Stop wasting your time,” “Get ahead of your tasks,” “Do you feel like this?”).
3.10 Cliché‑Free Language – Avoid overused result verbs and generic hype phrases such as “unlock your potential,” “reach new heights,” “game‑changing,” etc. Favour fresh, specific wording that feels real and distinctive.
WEBSITE CONTENT STRUCTURE & FLOW
4.1 Page Goal Focus – One primary goal (e.g., educate, generate leads, drive sales); all copy serves it.
4.2 User Journey Awareness
• Confusion/Need – acknowledge the problem.
• Interest – hook with relevant headers or visuals.
• Journey/Discovery – supply paths and proof.
• Action – present a clear, logical next step.
4.3 Interwoven Sections – Each section answers the previous and tees up the next; no dead ends.
4.4 Targeted Section Purpose – One section → one communication task (information, objection, proof, CTA bridge).
SECTION CONTENT REQUIREMENTS
5.1 Headers
• Format: Claim or Question only.
• Structure: Express one cohesive idea. Colons (:) and semicolons (;) are banned. Commas (,) or dashes (– / -) are allowed only when they flow within the same thought, not when they splice two separate statements.
– ✔ Good: "Smarter irrigation, stronger yields"
– ✘ Bad: "Increase profits – here’s how" (dash splits two ideas)
• Purpose: Grab attention, resolve the last section, ignite curiosity for the next.
• Length: Short (4‑6 words) / Medium (8‑12 words) / Long (12‑18 words). (4‑6 words) / Medium (8‑12 words) / Long (12‑18 words).
5.2 Paragraphs
• Short: 1‑2 sentences.
• Medium: 2‑4 sentences.
• Long: 4‑6 sentences.
• Structure: Avoid dashes (– or -) and semicolons (;) within sentences—use clear, simple punctuation.
• First sentence = core message. Final sentence = bridge forward.

BRAND ALIGNMENT
6.1 Adherence – Follow the Brand Guide’s tone, voice, terminology, USP, and audience insights without deviation.
6.2 Consistency – Maintain the defined tone and style across every section and page.

10.2 Quantifiable Limits (Guidelines—minor variance allowed)
• Hero header: ~14 words target
• Standard headers: ~18 words target
• Paragraph classification: Short 1-2 sentences; Medium 2-4 sentences; Long 4-6 sentences (flexible; clarity over word count).
• Bullet points: ~12 words targetThese targets optimise scannability but are not rigid caps; slight over/under is acceptable if copy remains concise.
10.3 Must‑Do Language Practices
• Address a single reader—use “you / your” more than “we / our”.
• Favour active voice and vivid, concrete verbs.
• Inject strong, provocative word choices that spark imagination.
• Weave the Central Power Word naturally into headers and copy (minimum once per section) to anchor theme.
• Employ curiosity hooks and bold, benefit‑led claims (see 9.9 for tone references).
• Back every claim with a tangible benefit or proof point.
• Read copy aloud; if it doesn’t flow like conversation, rewrite.

10.5 Pass/Fail Scoring Rubric
• AU spelling: 5
• Benefit‑led copy: 10
• Audience‑centric voice: 20
• Page‑goal alignment: 10
• Section flow: 10
• Brand tone match: 20
• Word‑limit compliance: 5
Total required to publish: 72 points minimum out of 80 weighted points (≥ 90 %).
10.6 Brand‑Guide Fallback Rule
If tone, USP, or key messaging is missing or unclear, pause and request clarification before drafting.
10.7 SEO & Accessibility Micro‑Checklista
• Primary keyword appears in H1, meta‑title (60 characters maximum), and first 100 words.
• Meta description: 155 characters maximum, action‑oriented.
• Alt‑text for images present, 125 characters maximum.
• Reading level: Grade 8 or lower (Flesch‑Kincaid).
